Rules

  • Othello is played on an 8x8 board.

  • There are two players, one black (Player 1) and one white (Player 2).

  • Each player starts with two pieces of their color in the centermost four squares of the board in a checker pattern.

  • A valid move constitutes “jumping over” opposing pieces. When this happens, all opposing pieces that have been “jumped over” are switched to the other color.

  • If a player has no valid moves, that player is passed and the opposing player can make another move.

  • The game is played until all pieces of a certain colour are taken or neither player can make a valid move. In the case of no valid moves, the tiles are counted and the player with more pieces wins.

The Technical Stuff

        By now, you have probably seen my Tic-Tac-Toe AI that I created and submitted as part of my blog. While it was fun to make, it wasn’t very challenging to program. Overall, Tic-Tac-Toe’s game mechanics are very limited, so the AI was (probably) a bit over-engineered, and there wasn’t much in terms of creating the game itself.

        Initially, I believed that my goals in regards to this AI were accomplishable with a simple expansion of my old work. However, Othello is much more complex than Tic-Tac-Toe. If you recall, the algorithm I used previously involved searching every single game state possible to find the most beneficial choice to make*.

This is fine for a game with a very small state-space complexity, but Othello is 25 orders of magnitude greater in that regard. This made it infeasible to generate an entire map of every game state possible. Due to the lack of perfect look-ahead, I needed to come up with something to compensate.

Alpha-Beta Pruning

        This strategy involves increasing the efficiency of the depth-first-search algorithm conducted in order to map out the search space. By deducing the relative value of branches that haven’t been searched yet, the algorithm is able to prevent any unnecessary searching.

Heuristic Analysis

        Since my search algorithm can’t map out the entire state-space, it is necessary to define a maximum “depth” that can be searched. If this was not implemented, it could take the AI potentially hours, days, or months to make a move. But since the AI can’t see every possible outcome, it is necessary to provide it incentive outside of winning or losing. What this means is that if the search algorithm reaches the maximum depth, it will simply return a score about the game-state at that level. I decided to score game-states based off of three criteria in ascending order of importance: # of pieces of AI’s color, # of pieces of AI’s color on edges, and # of pieces of AI’s color in corners. It is also worth noting that the score is subtracted if the opposing side has these favourable attributes.

* This choice assumes the opponent is perfectly logical. Depending on the implementation of the Mini-max algorithm, this assumption can hurt the success of the AI.

Why Did I Do This to Myself?

        Besides the very clever Shakespeare joke, I chose this game because it is on the precipice of being too complex for my barely-competent programming.

        The connection to my main ISU book, “I, Robot” by Isaac Asimov is also undeniable. There are many logic problems defined in the book that are solved (and sometimes caused) with highly specialized, logic driven AI such as this one. In fact, at the time of writing, this type of AI was a fairly new prospect. Making something that directly connects with the author of a book I absolutely loved was a very novel concept for me.

        Another connection is made to my main non-fiction book, “Superintelligence, Paths, Dangers, Strategies” by Nick Bostrom. While I would definitely not count my AI as a “Superintelligence”, it did make the concept much more visceral. If a simple AI made by me in two weeks could beat me every time I played against it, then what could stop humans from creating an intelligence that is better than us? Before I got into making simple AI’s, I was someone who agreed fully with the sentiment of QT-1, a robot from “I, Robot” who believed that the proposition “no being can create another being superior to itself” was self evident. Now, I am not so sure.

        All in all, making this game and AI has brought the subjects of my passion project into a clearer light. I more deeply connect with the themes of obsolescence and inferiority presented in my books, and I feel that taking the time to make something I enjoy was a pleasant break from the usual daily grind of school work.
